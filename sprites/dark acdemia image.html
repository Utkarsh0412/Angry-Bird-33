Of Romance, Literature, and Aesthetics: Inside the Cult of Dark Academia

By DU Beat September 7, 2020
Share
Share on Facebook Share on Twitter Email

Classic prose, dark coffee, Sapphic romances and pervasive sadness, this is the cult of Dark Academia.

Dark Academia – one of the internet’s manifold subcultures – seems to be quickly gaining currency with mainstream users. By the publication of this article, the Dark Academia hashtag has amassed over 187,255 posts on Instagram and 20 million views on TikTok.

But what is Dark Academia?

According to Wikia – the authoritative source on fandoms and niches – Dark Academia, abbreviated DA, is defined as ‘an aesthetic that revolves around classic literature, the pursuit of self-discovery, and a general passion for knowledge and learning.’

It is essentially an online community of people who are united by their shared interests in academia, vintage fashion and classic literature. Members of this community like to share content that is specific to their idea of the ‘picturesque’. Some of the most popular users who post DA-themed content are henrydefencesquad and dark.academia on Instagram with up to 18.1k and 15.6k followers respectively. Both having around 400 posts on average, their content variably offers screencaps from Eric Rohmer films, classical paintings, Greek sculpture and close-ups of darkened coffee. More interestingly, there are text-posts that are characterised by excessive nostalgia, teenage angst and poetic ambitions.

Dark Academia and Fashion

Another major component of the subculture is its refined sense of fashion. The active members of this community like to share look-books or ideas for DA-inspired outfits. The DA fashion heavily borrows elements from the 1930-40s fashion of major European cities. Some staples of the aesthetic are tweed blazers, plaid pants, black turtlenecks and sophisticated accessories.

Besides the elaborate aesthetic, DA has many specific folkways of day-to-day living: ‘make yourself tea in pretty teacups’, ‘host an unceasing bacchanal for you and your pals’ and – my favourite – ‘brood during a thunderstorm, and write extravagant, flowery poetry on parchment’. It goes without saying that DA has become a badge of identity, a secretive clique, a necessary diversion for many. The essence of DA is – very simply – its love for life.

Demographically, people who identify with the DA subculture can be approximated to be within the 14 to 25-year-old age group. They are more likely to be female and pursuing a secondary or college-level education in the humanities. Some members describe the DA demographic as ‘ a fusion between Slytherin and Ravenclaw sensibilities’. If those specifics seem to align with your details, then chances are you have already made your first contact with this community.

Being a college student of English literature, my Instagram feed was algorithmically infiltrated by Dark Academia posts around last summer. It was everywhere. An excerpt of Emily Dickinson poem set to the pictorial background of Gothic ruins. Old classics stacked together alongside a black coffee. Old tweed jackets hanging upon rustic bicycles. Wilden witticisms set to the image of cobblestone streets of a European city. You name it.

In my early Tumblr days, I had already been familiarised with a proto-version of this community — a sort of intermingling between the studyblr and early 2000s Goth aesthetic. But Dark Academia then was not what it has become today — a virtually inescapable, firmly established way of life. There are many possible reasons for the growing popularity of the DA subculture. For one, it is not as strenuous to maintain as its counterparts like ‘cottage core’ (an aesthetic that romanticizes life in the countryside) which initially requires extensive capital and a footloose job. With DA, one can just put on a vintage blouse, read Jane Austen and viola!

According to a recent study, this sudden interest in DA can also be a lockdown phenomenon. With schools and colleges across the country remaining closed, people are trying to emulate their academic lives online — a sort of self-deluding performance. But it isn’t just nostalgia for a pre-lockdown life; it is rooted way deeper into the past. The DA community identifies with distinctive styles from different periods in history — Greek civilization of antiquity, Gothic architecture, the 1930s-40s and Romantic literature.

Some popular figures within the community are Oscar Wilde (many claim to be ‘extra’ specifically to appease his ghost), Mary Shelley and John Keats. However, this doesn’t mean that DA does not use any contemporary references — Hozier, Lana Del Rey and Lorde are all parts of the canon. In fact, any current singer with a lyrically crafted discography including allusions to iconographies of the past is a suitable choice.

https://galaxxiesfaraway.tumblr.com/post/626895130541047808/modern-songs-with-dark-academia-vibes

Although the DA canon keeps expanding,  the primary sensibilities of the subculture have been mainly influenced by three cultural works – The Secret History (the first novel by American writer, Donna Tart), Dead Poets Society (1989) and Kill Your Darlings (2013).  These works were specifically chosen for their ominous narratives in academic settings. The Secret History is a ‘campus novel’ that relates the story of four-to-five uber-precocious, elite classics student in a New England college who get involved in a murder. A masterful blend of academia and macabre, the book is widely credited with creating the literary genre of DA. It is rife with sharply-etched visualizations that possibly served as a roadmap for the DA aesthetic:

“Though not untidy, exactly, it verged on being so. Books were stacked on every available surface; the tables were cluttered papers, ashtrays, bottles of whiskey, boxes of chocolates; umbrellas and galoshes made passage difficult in the narrow hall… Camilla’s night table was littered with empty teacups, leaky pens, dead marigolds in a water glass, and at the foot of her bed was a half-played game of solitaire… everywhere I looked was some fresh oddity: an old stereopticon, arrowheads in a dusty glass case, a staghorn fern, a bird’s skeleton…”

To further assess what Dark Academia actually represents, I briefly interviewed Sarah – a long-time blogger of DA content on Tumblr.

To me, Dark Academia is just… a really special way of living. I mean, it really isn’t even about anything else than a state of mind, right? It’s like you learn to romanticize and look forward to everyday things – riding the subway, or going to class, studying by your desk, waking up and getting coffee. All these otherwise ordinary tasks become like talismans of a special story. It’s a special kind of enjoyment in life. Being honest, it really helped me with a tough period. It is a form of escapism – but then again, what isn’t?

Sarah, a Dark Academic long-time blogger

Many people have criticized DA for being escapist, elitist and exclusionary. But there has been a conscious tendency to include other cultures in this inherently Eurocentric worldview. Contrary to what is being said, DA is actively working to diversify academic spaces than has been historically white, male and straight. With wider participation, DA is becoming more inclusive of ‘Oriental’ and South Asian culture. It also emphasizes – maybe a little too much – on queer identity. Besides all of that, it has also made the lives and works of classic authors more accessible to laypersons.

The type of thinking DA exemplifies — one of deliberate romanticism — seems to be a countercultural response to late capitalism and modern technology. Of course, it can be arguably simpler than that – and more complex at the same time. But there’s no reason — none that I can possibly think of — to actively denigrate the Dark Academia subculture. It has come as a necessary antithesis to the utilitarian approach to education – and more broadly, living – in our times.
